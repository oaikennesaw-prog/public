Intro

“Articles of Confederation and perpetual Union between the States of Newhampshire, Massachusetts-bay, Rhodeisland and Providence Plantations, Connecticut, New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Delaware, Maryland, Virginia, North Carolina, South Carolina, and Georgia.”

Intro

The Articles start with a list of names of the states. This observation suggests that the Articles place greater emphasis on the individual states rather than on the authority of the central government.

I

The stile of this confederacy shall be ‘‘The United States of America”.

I

The Articles of Confederation named the country "The United States of America" and formed its first government.

III

The said states hereby severally enter into a firm league of friendship with each other…

III

The opening of Article III characterizes the central government as a "league of friendship" and portrays it as an alliance intended to provide mutual support, while allowing each state to independently manage its internal affairs.

II

Each state retains its sovereignty, freedom and independence, and every power, jurisdiction and right, which is not by this confederation expressly delegated to the United States in Congress assembled.

II

Article II outlines the central theme of the document, emphasizing that primary power and authority are reserved for the states. It stipulates that any powers not explicitly granted to the central government remain with the states.

XI

Canada acceding to this confederation, and joining in the measures of the united states, shall be admitted into, and entitled to all the advantages of this union: but no other colony shall be admitted into the same, unless such admission be agreed to by nine states.

XI

Canada received pre-approved admission as the colonists aimed to unify North American territory and establish a "league of friendship" to safeguard against British advances from the north.

IV

Full faith and credit shall be given in each of these states to the records, acts and judicial proceedings of the courts and magistrates of every other state.

IV

Although this notes that States were required to recognize and honor other state’s laws, without a national court system, there was no neutral arbiter to settle disputes when states interpreted laws differently or ignored the "full faith and credit" clause. Each state functioned as its own ultimate legal authority.

IV

If any Person guilty of, or charged with, treason, felony, or other high misdemeanor in any state, shall flee from Justice, and be found in any of the united states, he shall upon demand of the Governor or executive power of the state from which he fled, be delivered up, and removed to the state having jurisdiction of his offence.

IV

This states that if someone is accused of a serious crime and escaped to another state, they would be returned when requested by the Governor. However, because there was no executive branch at the national level, these requirements between states could not be enforced. If a state chose not to extradite an individual, there was no central power to make them comply.

VI

No state without the Consent of the united states in congress assembled, shall send any embassy to, or receive any embassy from, or enter into any conference, agreement, alliance or treaty with any King, prince or state;… No two or more states shall enter into any treaty, confederation or alliance whatever between them, without the consent of the united states in congress assembled … No state shall lay any imposts of duties … No vessels of war shall be kept up in time of peace by any state, except such number only, as shall be deemed necessary by the united states in congress assembled … No state shall engage in any war without the consent of the united states in congress assembled

VI

The selected passage does not cover all the powers but the major ones including: policies related to foreign affairs and defense.

IV

…shall enjoy therein all the privileges of trade and commerce, subject to the same duties, impositions and restrictions as the inhabitants thereof respectively, provided that such restrictions shall not extend so far as to prevent the removal of property imported into any state, to any other State of which the Owner is an inhabitant; provided also that no imposition, duties or restriction shall be laid by any state, on the property of the united states, or either of them.

IV

Although this article promised "privileges of trade," states often ignored this by imposing their own tariffs, duties, and restrictions on out-of-state goods, which the central government lacked the power to regulate.

V

No state shall be represented in Congress by less than two, nor by more than seven Members; and no person shall be capable of being a delegate for more than three years in any term of six years; … In determining questions in the united states, in Congress assembled, each state shall have one vote.

V

The structure of government defined by the Articles of Confederation includes only a legislative branch. The judiciary was handled with state courts. Congress would elect a member to preside over meetings, but there was no presidency or executive branch.

IX

The united states in congress assembled, shall have the sole and exclusive right and power of determining on peace and war … The united states in congress assembled shall also have the sole and exclusive right and power of regulating the alloy and value of coin struck by their own authority.

IX

Even though Congress could coin money, it could not raise revenue through taxation. Only the states could levy and collect taxes. Congress did issue money (the Continental Currency), but many of the states issued their own currency as well. This lack of consistency in currency made trade between states and internationally confusing and led to economic difficulties for all.

XII

All bills of credit emitted, monies borrowed and debts contracted by, or under the authority of congress, before the assembling of the united states, in pursuance of the present confederation, shall be deemed and considered as a charge against the united states, for payment and satisfaction whereof the said united states, and the public faith are hereby solemnly pledged.

XII

The article highlighted that the new government expected to keep its financial promises. It was, however, essentially bankrupt because the central government did not have the power to tax individuals or control commerce, making it unable to uphold its commitment in this article.

XIII

And the Articles of this confederation shall be inviolably observed by every state, and the union shall be perpetual; nor shall any alteration at any time hereafter be made in any of them; unless such alteration be agreed to in a congress of the united states, and be afterward confirmed by the legislatures of every state.

XIII

The document required unanimous consent from all states in order to make any alteration to the document. This makes it extremely difficult to get the document changed so that it worked more effectively. Requiring all thirteen states to agree to changes made it nearly impossible to fix problems or adapt the system.

V

In determining questions in the united states, in Congress assembled, each state shall have one vote.

V

Giving each state one vote, regardless of population, created an unequal power distribution. Larger states like Virginia and Pennsylvania saw it as unfair that smaller states had equal voting power, which often resulted in legislative deadlocks and hindered progress on national issues.
